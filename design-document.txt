FractionBuddy
High School Fraction Intervention MVP
This document synthesizes the psychological, emotional, and functional requirements for a high school math intervention tool. It is intended to guide an AI agent in making design decisions that respect the unique sensitivities of older students working on elementary-level skills.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. Audience Identity & Skill Gap
The Profile: Bright high school students (9th and 10th graders) who are blocked from progressing into Algebra because they lack foundational 4th-grade fraction comparison skills.
The Knowledge Gap: These students often "don't know what they don't know". They may believe they understand fractions but rely on incorrect mental shortcuts—such as assuming a larger denominator always means a larger fraction or only comparing numerators.
The Skill Focus: The intervention must address four distinct comparison types: same denominator, same numerator, comparing against benchmarks (like 1/2), and unlike fractions.
2. The Psychological Landscape: Shame and Stigma
Internalized Failure: Many of these students have been told they are "bad at math" for years and have internalized this identity, leading them to "run out the clock" until graduation.
The Shame Factor: Students carry deep shame because they know they are working on "kid stuff". This manifests as physical withdrawal (putting heads down, shrinking) when fractions are introduced.
Social Vulnerability: The absolute greatest risk is peer discovery. A student may be making real progress but will stop immediately if a friend sees them doing what looks like elementary-level work.
The "Denial" Defense: To protect their ego, some students will claim they "already know this" and are just "rushing" or "making dumb mistakes".
3. Motivational Drivers: Proof Over Play
The Need for Tangible Progress: These students are skeptical of "gamification" (leaderboards, XP, rewards), which can feel patronizing. Instead, they respond to proof of growth. Seeing a data-driven comparison of their performance (e.g., "Last week you got 4/10, today you got 7/10") is a powerful motivator.
Momentum: Simple mechanics like streak counters and progress bars are effective for maintaining momentum without being distracting.
Autonomy: Students are more likely to engage if the tool doesn't feel like a "special" intervention group, which can feel stigmatizing.
4. Non-Obvious Design Considerations
An AI agent unfamiliar with this specific demographic might default to "educational" or "gamified" tropes. To succeed, the design must adhere to these directives:
Aesthetic Maturity (The "Anti-Kiddie" Rule):
Avoid: Bright "primary" colors, cartoon characters, playful fonts, or animations that feel juvenile.
Include: A "mature" UI that looks like a high-level productivity tool or a standard high school app. If it looks like it was made for a 10-year-old, they will refuse to use it.
Implicit Diagnostic:
Do not start with a formal, scary "Diagnostic Test". Instead, "fold" the assessment into the initial practice flow. The system should use the first few problems to identify gaps behind the scenes.
Instructional Integration (Drill that Teaches):
The experience should feel like a "drill" where they are "just doing problems".
When a student gets a problem wrong, the system must immediately provide a strategy-based visual model (number lines, worked examples) specific to that error type. It is not enough to just show the "right answer".
Discretion by Design:
The UI should be designed so that a casual observer (a peer walking by) cannot immediately tell the student is doing 4th-grade math. Minimalist layouts that focus on the "problem" rather than "level 1: fractions" titles are preferred.
Efficiency and Fluency:
The ultimate goal is to move the student from "reasoning through each step" to "internalized fluency" where they can solve comparisons in under 10 seconds.
5. MVP Feature Priority
Adaptive Practice Flow: The core experience where difficulty adjusts based on performance.
Embedded Strategy Instruction: Immediate, visual feedback for incorrect answers.
Basic Progress Tracking: A simple streak counter and progress bar to provide a sense of momentum.
Discreet, Mature UI: High-fidelity, professional-looking interface to mitigate social shame.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
UX Approach to follow: The "Conceptual Lab" (Strategy-First Exploration)
Core Idea and Mechanics: This philosophy rejects the idea of "drilling" and instead treats fraction comparison as a series of logic puzzles. The focus is on the "why" rather than the "what."
The Mechanic: Students don't just pick ">" or "<". They might be asked to "Prove it" by dragging fractions onto a number line or selecting the correct visual benchmark (e.g., "Is this closer to 0 or 1/2?").
Active Diagnostics: Instead of a hidden diagnostic, the app presents "Challenge Problems" that are designed to expose common mental shortcuts, like assuming a larger denominator means a larger fraction.
Strategy Feedback: The "instruction" is the core of the experience. Feedback isn't a "correction" but a "strategy reveal" that uses interactive visual models.
Why it Suits the Audience
Disrupting Shortcuts: It forces students to stop using incorrect mental "shortcuts" (comparing only numerators) by making them engage with the visual concept of the fraction.
Confidence Building: By mastering the "logic" behind the math, students can move away from the identity of being "bad at math".
Engagement: Frames the work as "problem-solving" rather than "remedial 4th-grade math," which feels more age-appropriate.
